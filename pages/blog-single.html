How Kingdoms of Ash
Hit 847% on Day One

Marcus Reid
May 2, 2026 · 8 min read

When Ember & Forge Studio launched Kingdoms of Ash on TabletopForge, nobody expected what happened next. Within the first six hours, the campaign had blown past its $10,000 goal — and by midnight, it was sitting at a jaw-dropping 847% funding.

We sat down with the studio's founder, Callum Marsh, to break down exactly what they did in the weeks leading up to launch — and what every first-time campaign team can learn from their experience.

1. They Built the Audience Before the Campaign

Three months before launch, Ember & Forge started a weekly campaign diary on TabletopForge's community forum. They shared prototype images, asked for feedback on mechanics, and ran naming polls for factions. By the time they launched, they had over 2,000 people who already felt personally invested in the game.

"We didn't launch to strangers. We launched to a community we'd spent months building. That changes everything about what day one looks like."

2. The Page Design Did the Heavy Lifting

Their campaign page was strikingly different from the typical wall-of-text. Every section answered one question: "Why should I back this?" The game's core tension was explained in a single paragraph. Pledge tiers were named after in-universe titles. The art direction matched the game's tone exactly.

3. They Mastered the 48-Hour Window

Crowdfunding campaigns live and die in their first 48 hours. Kingdoms of Ash was designed for maximum momentum in this window — early bird tiers with genuine value, a launch-day stretch goal that seemed achievable, and a coordinated social push across Discord, BGG, and Reddit.

The result? 1,200 backers on day one. Algorithms noticed. Organic discovery kicked in. The campaign essentially funded itself from day three onwards.

What You Can Take Away

Not every campaign will hit 847%. But the fundamentals are repeatable: build your audience early, design for clarity, and engineer the first 48 hours to create momentum. TabletopForge is built to help you do all three.
